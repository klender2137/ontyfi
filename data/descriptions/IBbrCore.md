# Investment Banking



**ID:** IB-core

**Branch:** M&A / Investment Banking

### Introduction

Every year **more than $5 trillion worth of mergers and acquisitions** are advised by investment banks worldwide. Global banks also help companies raise **over $3 trillion in bonds and equity capital annually**, while trading desks process **hundreds of billions of dollars in securities every day** across global markets. Behind these huge numbers sits an industry that acts as the financial architect of the corporate world.

Investment banking is a branch of financial services that helps companies, governments, and institutions **raise capital, execute large financial transactions, and access global markets**. It connects businesses that need funding with investors who have capital to deploy. Investment banks also trade securities, analyze markets, and structure complex financial products used to manage risk or finance expansion.

---

# Main Functions of the Investment Banking Industry

### • Capital Raising & Financial Structuring

Investment banks help companies raise money through equity and debt markets. When a firm needs large amounts of funding, bankers structure securities and place them with institutional investors. They design financing solutions such as **corporate bonds, leveraged loans, or private equity placements** depending on the company's credit profile and growth plans. Teams also coordinate roadshows, investor meetings, and pricing of securities before they are issued. This function allows companies to finance acquisitions, build factories, refinance debt, or expand globally while investors gain access to structured investment opportunities.

### • Mergers, Acquisitions & Strategic Advisory

One of the most visible parts of investment banking is advising companies during **mergers, acquisitions, or corporate restructuring**. Bankers analyze companies, value businesses, and negotiate deal structures between buyers and sellers. They build financial models to estimate synergies, transaction costs, and long term returns for shareholders. During a deal they coordinate lawyers, accountants, and executives while managing the negotiation process until the transaction closes.

### • Sales, Trading & Market Liquidity

Investment banks run large trading desks that help institutional clients buy and sell securities such as stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities. Traders provide liquidity by constantly quoting prices and executing large orders for clients like hedge funds, pension funds, and asset managers. Because many of these orders are huge, traders split them into smaller transactions to avoid moving the market. Banks also earn revenue through **market making**, where they profit from the difference between buying and selling prices while keeping markets liquid.

### • Research & Market Intelligence

Investment banks publish detailed research on companies, industries, and global economic trends. Equity and fixed income analysts build valuation models, examine financial statements, and publish reports that help investors decide whether to buy, sell, or hold securities. These reports often include forecasts for earnings, interest rates, or industry growth. Research teams also support bankers internally by providing insights about sectors such as technology, energy, or healthcare before potential deals.

### • Risk Management & Regulatory Compliance

Modern investment banks run complex risk management systems to protect themselves and their clients from extreme financial losses. Teams monitor market exposure using models such as stress tests and scenario analysis. They evaluate how sudden changes in interest rates, currency values, or commodity prices could impact trading portfolios. Banks also implement compliance systems to detect fraud, money laundering, or market manipulation, ensuring that operations follow global financial regulations.

# Entry Paths Into Investment Banking

### Finance Graduate Analyst
Many juniors enter investment banking after completing a **degree in finance, economics, or business**. During university they often join finance clubs, participate in case competitions, and learn Excel based financial modeling. These students usually discover the field through business courses or investment banking career events. They enter banks through **summer internships that convert into full time analyst roles**.

### Quantitative / Math Graduate
Some juniors come from **mathematics, statistics, or engineering degrees**. They develop strong analytical skills and programming knowledge used in financial modeling and trading. Many discover investment banking through quantitative finance courses or internships in trading desks. They often join roles related to derivatives, risk modeling, or structured products.

### MBA Career Switcher
Professionals sometimes transition into investment banking after completing an **MBA program**. These candidates usually have previous experience in consulting, corporate finance, or industry operations. During their MBA they network with banks and secure associate level roles after internship programs.

### Self Taught Finance Enthusiast
A smaller but growing path involves students who discover investment banking through **online financial modeling courses, finance communities, or investing blogs**. They build skills independently in Excel, accounting, and valuation techniques. These candidates often start with internships at smaller boutique banks or corporate finance advisory firms.

# Market Structure: Types of Investment Banking Institutions

### Bulge Bracket Banks
Bulge bracket banks are the largest global investment banks and operate across every major financial market. They handle the biggest deals in the world and provide a full range of services including M&A advisory, underwriting, trading, and asset management. Examples include firms such as Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley.

### Elite Boutique Banks
Elite boutiques are smaller advisory focused banks that specialize mainly in **M&A and restructuring deals**. They usually do not operate large trading businesses but compete directly with large banks on major corporate transactions. Their teams are often highly specialized and known for deep advisory expertise.

### Middle Market Banks
Middle market banks advise companies on deals typically **between $100 million and $1 billion**. They provide many of the same services as larger banks but focus on mid sized companies rather than global corporations. These firms are often strong in regional markets or specific industries.

### Industry Boutique Banks
Industry boutiques focus on a **single sector such as healthcare, technology, or energy**. Because of this specialization they often have deeper knowledge of specific markets and relationships with companies in those sectors. They usually work on smaller but highly specialized transactions.

# Summary

Investment banking is the industry that helps companies raise capital, execute mergers, and access global financial markets. Banks combine advisory work, trading operations, and financial research to connect businesses with investors and manage large financial transactions. For ambitious students interested in corporate strategy, financial modeling, and global markets, it remains one of the most demanding but influential careers in finance.
